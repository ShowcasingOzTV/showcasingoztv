Hay (NSW)

Hay is a historic outback town in the Riverina region of south-western New South Wales, located on the banks of the Murrumbidgee River. Known for its vast open plains and strong pastoral heritage, Hay has long been a key centre for agriculture and transport across inland Australia.

Established in the mid-19th century, Hay developed as an important river crossing and supply point for surrounding pastoral stations. The town became a major hub for the wool industry, with sheep grazing and shearing forming the backbone of its early economy and identity.

The surrounding Hay Plains are among the flattest landscapes in Australia, creating expansive horizons and a distinctive outback atmosphere. These wide-open spaces have shaped both the lifestyle and character of the region, contributing to Hay’s reputation as a quintessential inland town.

The Murrumbidgee River continues to play a central role in the town, supporting irrigation, recreation, and community life. Riverside areas provide opportunities for walking, fishing, and relaxing in a natural setting.

Today, Hay offers a range of services, accommodation, and visitor facilities, with heritage buildings, museums, and public spaces reflecting its rich history. The town remains an important service centre for surrounding agricultural districts.

Surrounded by open plains, river landscapes, and rural properties, Hay captures the scale and simplicity of outback New South Wales. The town reflects resilience, history, and a strong connection to land and water.
