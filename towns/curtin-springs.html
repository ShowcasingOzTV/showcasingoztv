Curtin Springs (NT)

Curtin Springs is a remote outback station and roadhouse in the Northern Territory, located along the Lasseter Highway between Erldunda and Uluru. Set within the vast landscapes of Australia’s Red Centre, Curtin Springs serves as both a working cattle station and a key stop for travellers exploring the region.

Established as a pastoral lease in the mid-20th century, Curtin Springs developed to support cattle grazing across expansive arid land. The station continues to operate as a family-run enterprise, reflecting the resilience and adaptability required to manage large-scale pastoral operations in one of Australia’s most challenging environments.

Curtin Springs plays an important role as a service hub in a remote part of the Northern Territory, providing fuel, accommodation, meals, and supplies for travellers journeying between major destinations such as Uluru, Kata Tjuta, and Kings Canyon. Its roadhouse atmosphere captures the practical and welcoming nature of outback travel.

The surrounding landscape is defined by red earth, open plains, and distant mesas, offering a classic Red Centre experience. One of the most prominent natural features nearby is Mount Conner, often mistaken for Uluru, which rises dramatically from the surrounding desert.

Today, Curtin Springs offers a unique blend of pastoral life and tourism, allowing visitors to experience both the working station environment and the vastness of central Australia. Its location makes it an ideal base for exploring the region’s iconic landmarks while enjoying a quieter and more authentic outback setting.

Surrounded by expansive desert landscapes and big outback skies, Curtin Springs captures the scale, isolation, and beauty of the Northern Territory interior. The station reflects the enduring spirit of remote Australia and the people who live and work there.

Mount Conner Lookout

Mount Conner Lookout provides views of the distinctive flat-topped mesa known as Mount Conner, a striking geological formation rising from the desert plains. Often mistaken for Uluru, the site highlights the scale and unique landforms of the Red Centre landscape.
