White Cliffs (NSW)

White Cliffs is a remote outback town in far western New South Wales, best known for its opal mining history, underground living, and vast desert landscapes. Located north-east of Wilcannia, White Cliffs offers a distinctive and authentic outback experience shaped by both necessity and innovation.

Established in the late 19th century following the discovery of opal, White Cliffs quickly became one of Australia’s earliest opal mining centres. The harsh climate and extreme heat led many residents to construct underground homes, known as “dugouts,” which remain a defining feature of the town today.

These underground dwellings provide natural insulation, maintaining comfortable temperatures year-round and offering visitors a unique insight into life in one of Australia’s most challenging environments.

White Cliffs is also notable for its role in renewable energy history, having been home to one of the world’s first solar power stations. This innovation reflects the town’s ability to adapt and find practical solutions in a remote setting.

Surrounded by arid plains, low scrub, and expansive skies, the landscape around White Cliffs is both stark and striking. The sense of isolation and scale is a key part of its appeal, attracting travellers seeking a genuine outback experience.

Accommodation in White Cliffs often reflects the town’s character, with options ranging from underground stays to small motels and caravan facilities, providing a memorable and distinctly local experience.

White Cliffs captures the resilience, creativity, and resourcefulness of outback Australia — a place where history, environment, and innovation come together in a truly unique way.
