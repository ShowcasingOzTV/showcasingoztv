Coober Pedy (SA)

Coober Pedy is one of Australia’s most distinctive outback towns, located in the far north of South Australia along the Stuart Highway. Renowned as the world’s opal capital, the town is famous for its underground homes, known as dugouts, which provide relief from the extreme desert temperatures and offer a fascinating insight into life in the arid interior.

The discovery of opals in 1915 shaped Coober Pedy’s identity, drawing prospectors from around the world and creating a multicultural community built on mining, resilience, and ingenuity. Today, visitors can explore active and historic opal fields, underground museums, and working mines that tell the story of the town’s colourful past.

Coober Pedy’s underground lifestyle extends beyond housing, with underground churches, hotels, shops, and galleries carved into the rock. These unique spaces showcase the creativity of the town’s residents and have become major attractions for travellers seeking an experience unlike anywhere else in Australia.

Surrounded by striking desert landscapes, Coober Pedy offers access to natural attractions such as the Breakaways Conservation Park, Moon Plain, and colourful mesas that have featured in numerous films and television productions. The otherworldly terrain and vast open skies make the region particularly memorable for photography and sightseeing.

As a key stop for travellers crossing Australia from north to south, Coober Pedy continues to thrive as a gateway to the outback, combining rich mining heritage, unique architecture, and unforgettable desert scenery. The town remains a symbol of adaptability and adventure, offering visitors a truly iconic Australian outback experience.

Spaceship from Pitch Black

Johnston Geodetic Station

Dog Fence

The Dog Fence near Coober Pedy forms part of the world’s longest continuous fence, built to protect livestock from wild dogs. It stands as an impressive example of large-scale rural infrastructure and outback problem-solving.

Marla

Marla is a small outback settlement in South Australia located along the Stuart Highway, serving as an important stopover for travellers heading between Coober Pedy and the Northern Territory. The town functions primarily as a roadhouse service centre, providing fuel, accommodation, and essential supplies in a remote and sparsely populated region. Surrounded by arid landscapes and vast open plains, Marla reflects the practical, no-frills nature of life in Australia’s interior.
